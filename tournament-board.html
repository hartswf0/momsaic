How the simulation works ratings from what actually happened

Every game is playable through one model. Tap a bracket tie or a group fixture, or run the whole tournament — all of it calls the same match engine, the way the live match on the main tool shares its ratings with the forecast.

The ratings are earned, not assumed. Each nation's attack is its goals scored per game and its defence its goals conceded, taken from every match it has actually played in this tournament — group and knockout — then shrunk toward the tournament average by a sample-trust dial, because a few games is thin evidence. There are no reputations or world rankings here: a side is exactly as good as its results.

A match is simulated by turning those two ratings into an expected-goals figure for each side — your attack lifted by how leaky their defence is, their attack lifted by yours — and drawing a scoreline from it. Knockout draws go to a coin flip weighted slightly by attacking strength. Run it thousands of times and the share of outcomes becomes the odds.

Two nations have no data. Uzbekistan and Panama played in groups whose scores were never reported, so they carry a neutral, average rating. Everything here is an estimate from one tournament of form, not a prediction you should bet on.

The bracket is set to the quarterfinals. Switzerland came through the last round-of-16 tie on penalties, so all four quarterfinals have their teams; the simulator rolls forward from there. Real kickoff times and venues are shown on each remaining tie.
