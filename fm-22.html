How it works two models, one set of ratings

The watch engine is a genuine recursive multi-agent simulation. Every tactical update runs a fixed pipeline on an immutable snapshot: each of 22 agents takes a bounded local observation, refines its action state through three inner recursive iterations, exchanges relation-conditioned messages with nearby players (a defender tells a teammate to drop and cover; a carrier tells a winger to make the run), then teams jointly allocate operational roles — exactly one primary challenger and one goal-cover each — and every agent scores its action candidates before physics moves anyone. Tap a player to watch all of it.

Player quality is real. Each agent's pace, finishing, passing and tackling come from the roster ratings derived from the stat sheet — so Havertz finishes, Brown threatens from left-back, and Côte d'Ivoire defend like a side that just kept a clean sheet.

The forecast is a Monte-Carlo readout. The full engine is too heavy to run thousands of times in a browser, so the Predict tab uses a fast match model built from the very same ratings — attacking quality, creation, finishing and defensive solidity per side — and simulates the match thousands of times. The verdict up top is the share of those outcomes. It is a prediction by simulation, not a formula, and it shares its inputs with the match you watch.

Honesty. Both teams have played one match. Ratings from a single game are uncertain, which is why the sample-trust dial exists and why every number here is an estimate, not a tip.
